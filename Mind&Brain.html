Scientific Method

- Dependent Variable - The variable to be measured
    - Direct - e.g Neural activity
    - Indirect - e.g Blood flow
- Independent Variable - A variable we can change reliably
    - e.g Environmental Housing, Objects

Other Methods

- Correlations
    - No independent variable
    - 2+ dependent variables, and studying the relationship
    - e.g Height vs Weight
    - Pearson's R; where R is the relation between these variables. R ranges from -1 to 1, 
    -1 being inversely proportional, +1 being proportional and 0 being unrelated.
    - Correlations *do not infer* causation
    - Experiments can be done by affecting one of the dependent variables and studying
    the differences observed in the other

Cerebral Spinal Fluid

* Found in ventricles and surrounding the brain and spinal cord in subarachnoid space
* A clear colourless liquid comprised of inorganic salts, glucose and proteins 
* Provides some nutrients, but primary job is to protect brain

* Cycles through 4 ventricles: 2 lateral, 3rd and 4th ventricle
* Produced in the 'choroid plexus'

Nerve Impulses & Cell Communication

Extracellular : Outside Cell Intracellular : Inside Cell Seawater and Extracellular fluid have roughly the same concentrations of Na+, Cl- and K+. In contrast, Intracellular fluid has very low amounts of Na+ and Cl-, but very high amounts of K+. This balance is maintained by the Cell Membrane; which is created with a Hydrophylic head and a Hydrophobic tail.

At rest, a neuron's Na+ and K+ channels are closed. Because of the charges inside the neuron, the neuron has a charge of -70 millivolts. K+ can leave because of the concentration gradient, but can also be absorbed because of the chemical gradient. Electroconvulsive Therapy is used to treat major depression, and involves two electrodes being placed on the side of the head. After a current is passed, the patient enters an induced seizure. Post seizure, the patient is unaware of what occurred, but depressive thoughts are decreased. Modern ECT can involve the use of seizure-suppresent medications or stimulations via magnetic tools.

    * VERY large peak

    * Lasts about 1 milisecond

Threshold
    * Voltage on a nueral membrane at which an action potential is triggered
    * ~-50 mV relative to extracellular surround
    * Sort of like trigger-weights on guns; the weight increases up to a point before the explosive action potential is released
    * Voltage sensitive channels open up at this ~-50 mV threshold
Refractory Period
    * Absolute
        * The depolarizing/repolarizing period
        * It can't get it up, regardless of the stimulation
    * Relative
        * With enough stimulation, the cell can fire again
        * Depolarizing/repolarizing still occurs
    * Acts like a debounce in circuitry or event based programming

Myelin Sheath

Speeds up neural impulse by insulating the axon
Action potentials do not occur over the sheath
Gaps in the sheath are called *Nodes of Ranvier*
These gaps contain huge amounts of voltage-sensitive channels

Synapse

Very small connection between neurons Speed of conduction along an axon is about 40 meters per second If many axons are strung together, this speed can reduce to 15m per second Axon Hillock are very rich in voltage sensitive channels where EPSP and IPSP summate. If the sum of the electrical activity reaches higher than -50mV, the Action Potential is triggered. Hillocks are located at the base of the axon

Summation

Temporal: Close in time Spatial: Close in space Essentially, the total electrical activity per a given location has a larger magnitude if the pulses are physically and timewise nearby. Spatial summation can also involve order, as the dendrites of a neuron contain many branches. If many signals reach the cell starting from the top and reaching towards the cell body, the signal is amplified more than if the inverse occurs.

The act of a neuron on the pre-synaptic side releasing a chemical (with an excitatory or inhibitory effect) to be used on the post-synaptic neuron. These chemicals are known as Neurotransmitters which are known as hormones when in the bloodstream.

Neurotransmission

1. Sythesis
    * Neurotransmitter is synthesized and stored in axon terminal
        * Good for smaller neurotransmitters

        * According to DNA and transported via microtubules to axon terminal
    * Synaptic vesicle stores the neurotransmitters, kind of like a bubble
    * MAO (monoamine oxidase) breaks down excess Neurotransmitters
        * MAO inhibitors yields more NT available, used (early on) for depression treatment
2. Action Potential
    * Propagates through the axon
3. Releases
    * NT is brought to the presynaptic membrane and released in an action potential 
    * Ca++ channels open, binding to a protein called calmodulin****
        * These calmodulin complexes cause some vesicles to burst and release the NT into the synaptic gap
    * This process is exocytosis**** (See image)
    * Via retrograde transport, the vesicle is reformed on the cell membrane
4. Receptors
    * NT activates the receptors on the postsynaptic membrane
    * Receptors either hyperpolarize, depolarize the cell or cause chemical reactions within the cell
    * There are some receptors located on the presynaptic side, generally used to "shut off" the flow of NT 
    * These are autoreceptors, generally used exclusively for one or two variants of neurotransmitter
5. Inactivations
    * The NT is deactivated, else the signal never stops working
    * This reset can be done via four methods: 
        * Diffusion: NT floats away from synapse
        * Degradation via enzymes in synaptic cleft
        * Reuptake**** into the presynaptic axon by transporters. Once returned they can be reused
        * Absorbed by neighboring glial cells and returned later
6. Feedback
    * Messages arrive to control release of NT by the presynaptic cell

    Hallucinations - Indistinguishable from reality for one experiencing it

    Disorganized behaviour

Type 1: Positive Symptoms
    Excess of symptoms and normative values, including agitated movements, exaggerated responses
    Some relation to the dopaminergic dysfunction
    Treatable with a favourable outcome
    Not always perfect, but antipsychotics do improve symptoms
Type 2: Negative Symptoms
    Deficient in regular behaviours, neurological issues
    Enlarged ventricles in the brain, increasing pressure and damaging the cortex
    Cortical atrophy, smaller proportions
    When measuring cerebral blood flow, prefrontal cortex activity is reduced
    Abnormalities in Wernicke's area, auditory areas, hippocampus and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex
Pyrimidal neurons in the hippocampus are generally aligned with one another
Schizophreniacs have slightly less ordered pyrimidal neurons
Dopaminergic receptors (in striatal areas (Striatum is a basal ganglia)) are increased

How do we see color? Trichromatic Theory explains this by breaking each color down into their RGB components. This does a great job explaining how colorblindness comes about, but fails to explain afterimages and other visual phenomena

Afterimages appear when "fatigued" cells continuously display the same color after use

Opponent-Process Theory emphasizes opposite colors, which are processed in retinal ganglion cells. On-Off center/surround : Each specific cone has the base receptor in the center, followed by a surrounding receptor for the opposing color. Lecture 11: Movement

Muscles: Divided into Skeletal/Striated (Move the limbs, striped appearance), Smooth (Organ movement and internal processes) and Cardiac. Muscles contract and relax, and are organized into pairs: Extensor and Flexor, which controls movement away from the body; E.G the bicep is a Flexor, making the forearm approach the body. The tricep works on the opposing side, bringing the forearm away from the body (Acting as an Extensor). In this way, the flexor and extensor are antagonistic, providing incredible control over limb placement and smooth movements. This combination also limits tremors and shaking.

Spinal Cord Reflexes depend on the spinal cord alone, such as scratch reflex and pain reception. Damage to the spinal cord can result in paralysis such as paraplegia or quadroplegia. Higher than C2 and C3 can cause problems relating to cardio control.

Motor Neurons in the spinal cord (along the Final Common Pathway) interface with interneurons and motor neurons to produce movement.

Reflexes can be divided up into Monosynaptic Reflexes, which require only one synapse in the ventral horn in the spinal cord before a signal is received, and multisynaptic reflexes, which rely on sensory data and information before proceding with an action.

Muscles also incorporate Proprioceptors, such as the Muscle Spindle (stretching) and Golgi Tendon Organ (Overextensions)

Central Pattern Generators is the idea that multiple monosynaptic reflexes can be combined into more complex movement including walking.
